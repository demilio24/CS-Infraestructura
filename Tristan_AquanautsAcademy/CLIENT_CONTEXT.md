CUSTOMER PAIN POINTS (from industry research)
-------------------------------------------
General private-swim parent (Vancouver Island, BC):
- Group lessons = ~20% instructor attention per child, ~5 min of actual swimming
- Plateau frustration: "She's done Level 3 three times"
- Instructor turnover at rec centres (teenage staff rotation)
- Fear / past trauma — child froze in busy public pool
- Drowning awareness: Island Health had 24 of BC's 98 accidental drowning deaths in 2024 (#2 health region in the province)
- Scheduling rigidity of 8-week sessions, conflicts with shift work + multiple kids

What they're paying for (ranked):
1. Water safety / "won't drown"
2. Confidence (the kid who jumps in vs. clings to the wall)
3. Fear resolution (often the trigger)
4. Skill progression / stroke development
5. Fitness / lifelong activity (secondary)

Triggers to move from group to premium private:
- A scary water event or near-miss
- Word of mouth from another parent
- Upcoming cottage/lake/cruise trip
- Plateau on a level

What makes them choose one provider over another:
- Instructor credentials (NLS, WSI, adaptive training) — parents do ask
- Mobile / in-home service (huge Island lever — many families drive 45+ min to rec centres)
- Sensory-friendly setting
- Online booking + flexible scheduling
- Sibling / family packages
- Reviews mentioning their specific child profile

Autistic / neurodiverse families specifically:
- Group lessons functionally fail this audience (sensory overload, pace mismatch, behavioural misreads)
- They need: pre-lesson intake, visual supports, predictable routines, comfort with non-speaking communication, AAC awareness, parallel co-regulation
- KILLER STAT: autistic children are **160x more likely to die from drowning than the general pediatric population** (Columbia Mailman, 2017, *AJPH*)
- Drowning is the #1 cause of death for children with ASD
- ~49% of autistic children attempt elopement (Pediatrics, 2012)
- 91% of US autism wandering fatalities in 2024 involved drowning (NAA)

This reframes adaptive swim from "nice-to-have" to literal life-safety equipment. It belongs above the fold on the adaptive page.

BC AUTISM FUNDING UNIT (AFU) — PRICE-OBJECTION KILLER
-------------------------------------------
Most adaptive-audience parents don't know they may be able to fund swim lessons through AFU. Aquanauts' own page only says "case by case" — this should be expanded into a real FAQ block.

How it works:
- BC Ministry of Children and Family Development, run by AFU
- Once child has confirmed ASD diagnosis, family receives annual allocation
- Parents pay provider, submit invoice to AFU portal for reimbursement, OR provider invoices AFU directly if registered

Funding amounts (verified, gov.bc.ca/autismfunding):
- Under age 6: up to $22,000/year
- Ages 6–18: up to $6,000/year
- Travel/Training/Equipment carve-out: up to 20% of annual allocation
- Submission deadline: 6 months from date of service

Does adaptive swim qualify?
- Not as pure "recreation lessons" (explicitly excluded by AFU)
- BUT qualifies via two well-documented routes:
  1. Delivered by a registered Behaviour Interventionist (BI) under RASP-supervised behaviour plan — AFU's own example language references swim lessons here
  2. As "specialized therapeutic activities and camps designed for children with autism" (eligible for 6-18 allocation)

What proof parents need:
- ASD diagnosis on file with AFU (one-time)
- Itemized invoice from provider — framed as skill-building / behaviour-plan-aligned (not "swim lessons")
- For BI route: paperwork tying sessions to behaviour plan + RASP supervisor relationship
- Written rationale recommended; AFU encourages pre-approval

Local precedent: AQUA S.T.A.R. (direct Vancouver Island competitor) publicly markets as an AFU-registered provider with intake flow + 50% upfront / 50% reimbursed model. Aquanauts can match or beat this.

Brandon Tolley on the Aquanauts team is already a behaviour interventionist — Aquanauts has an unusual unlock to register or partner for AFU billing.
